Both proposals and request for proposals can be used to initiate a project. An RFP is usually issued by an organization to solve a problem. The proposal itself initiates a particular solution for the project. A proposal is typically written against an RFP to acquire funding to solve the problem that particular project by the means written in your proposal. No RFP will be looking precisely for the idea you have formulated, so it is up to the writer to like the idea with the RFP &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01. Thesis and dissertations typically fall in the range of "short proposals", as they typically range from 2 to 10 pages.

1. Purpose of a Proposal

Any proposal has three main components &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01:

  • The work is significant, consistent with the state-of-the-art, fills clear needs
  • The person working the project is the right person
  • The project is feasible

A winning proposal not only gives the reader the confidence that the writers have thoroughly understood both the need that the project is addressing and possible solutions other than their own; it also conveys a clear sense that the writers are capable of successfully completing the project—that they are able to budget time and resources appropriately &Irish_Robert2013-04-01.

2. Style of Proposal

All proposals contain similar contents &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01:

  • Purpose is concisely stated at the beginning
  • Relevant background information is summarized
  • The language is understandable
  • The sentences are short to moderate to enhance readability
  • The proposed ideas are stated clearly
  • All the information provided convinces the reader that the project is worth funding (or accepting)

3. Preparing to Write

Once you know what is desired to be proposed:

  • State the purpose clearly
  • State explicitly the need of the proposal
  • Ensure that the work is consistent with the philosophy of those who will fund/approve
  • Ensure that the proposed work meets the desired need

Now that the problem has been identified, and a solution provided, how will the project be done? The outline should address &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01:

  • Method(s) and approach(es)
  • Timeline
  • Resources
  • Projected outcome
  • Assessment plan (how you will measure the project's success)

This "how" section is helpful to be represented as a table or Gantt chart.

4. Proposals

Many proposals will have a specified format which ought to be followed. In the absence of guidelines, the following structure may be suitable &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01;&Irish_Robert2013-04-01:

  • Cover Page
  • Executive Summary
    • Hypothesis or Purpose
    • Motivation and Scope
    • Objectives
  • The Narrative
    • State-Of-The-Art
    • Methodology
    • Procedure
  • The Budget (N/A for my case so I skip)
  • References or Bibliography
  • Supporting Documentation

4.1. Cover Page/Title Page

  • Title of project
  • Submitted to
  • Date
  • Name of principle investigators
  • Institutions to which the PI(s) belong

4.2. Project Overview/Executive Summary/Abstract:

The project overview/executive summary/abstract should be written last. It should be able to answer the following &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01:

  • What do you want to do?
  • How will it be done?
  • Who is going to do it?
  • How will it fill the role of the RFP/problem statement
  • What are the anticipated outcomes?

The reader will understand the originality, rationale, the expected outcomes of the proposed work, the scale of the project, the need for the work, and the significance of the outcome. If these are not portrayed clearly, then it must be revisited.

4.3. Narrative

This is the body of the text. It is typically subdivided into a few sections.

4.3.1. Introduction/Background/Need and Fit

This section is where a strong case is made for what the work is, how it fits with the state-of-the-art, why it is significant, and how it aligns with the RFP/problem statement. The section should include

  • State-Of-The-Art: what is known and what is lacking
  • Background providing perspective for the reader
  • Statement of need in the context of existing information
  • Fits with the RFP/problem statement
  • Purpose, goals, and measurable objectives
  • Compelling and logical reasons for support

4.3.2. Method

How will the project be accomplished, and why was that method chosen?

  • Experimental
  • Analytical
  • Computational
  • Combined

Describe the methods and processes. Why is the method appropriate? How will the work be validated? What are the outcomes?

4.3.3. Project Timeline

This includes mileposts for completing different stages of the project. The timeline will include

  • Shows project flow
  • Start and end dates
  • Lists a schedule of activities, including project reports
  • Describes projected outcomes

4.4. List of References/Bibliography

Cite, cite, cite.

5. Short Proposal Format

In academia, candidates for graduate degrees typically write formal proposals for their thesis or dissertation projects before officially beginning work on the projects. M.S. thesis proposals are typically 5 to 10 pages. For PhD dissertations proposals typically range from 10 to 15 pages; however, it is commonly seen that they range from 15 to 25 pages. A typical outline of a thesis/dissertation is outlined as follows &Knisely_Charles_W_2014-01-01:

  • Title Page: Proposed research topic, name, date, department, thesis advisor
  • Introduction - Problem to be addressed
    • Purpose of proposed work
    • Background and literature review, including theoretical analysis (if applicable)
    • Significance and scope of work
  • Methods for solving the problem
    • Plan for accomplishing objectives
    • Justification for instrumentation, and data acquisition and processing
    • Plan for evaluating results
    • Timetable for project completion
  • Resources
    • What is needed to solve the problem
  • References
