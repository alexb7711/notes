* Representation and approximation in vector spaces
** The approximation problem in Hilbert space
The analysis problem is this: given a vector $x \in S$, find the coefficients $c_1, c_2, ..., c_m$ that

\begin{equation*}
\hat{x} = c_1 p_1 + c_2 p_2 + \cdots + c_m p_m
\end{equation*}

Where the $\hat{\cdot}$ is used in indicate an approximation $x = \hat{x} + e$.

\begin{equation*}
||x - \hat{x}|| = ||e||
\end{equation*}

When using the $l_2$ or $L_2$ norm, this is called the least-squares problem. Because we know that the error is
minimized when the error is perpendicular to the data, we can leverage the inner-product (also the $l_2$ norm) to
minimize the problem by writing

\begin{equation*}
\Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, p_j \Big> \text{ for } 1,2,...,m
\end{equation*}

In matrix form this equates to

\begin{equation*}
\begin{bmatrix}
\langle p_1, p_1 \rangle & \langle p_2, p_1 \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_1 \rangle \\
\langle p_1, p_2 \rangle & \langle p_2, p_2 \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_2 \rangle \\
\vdots          &            &   & \vdots          \\
\langle p_1, p_m \rangle & \langle p_2, p_m \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_1 \rangle \\
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_m
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\langle x,p1 \rangle \\ \langle x,p2 \rangle \\ \vdots \\ \langle x,pm \rangle \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

*** The Grammian Matrix
Let $R$ be called the Grammian matrix.

\begin{equation*}
R =
\begin{bmatrix}
<p_1, p_1> & <p_2, p_1> & \cdots & <p_m, p_1> \\
<p_1, p_2> & <p_2, p_2> & \cdots & <p_m, p_2> \\
\vdots          &            &    & \vdots          \\
<p_1, p_m> & <p_2, p_m> & \cdots & <p_m, p_1> \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

The Grammian is a Hermitian symmetric matrix; that is $R^H = R$ where $\cdot^H$ is the conjugate transpose. This also
implies that $R$ is invertible. This brings us to the following theorem

#+name theorem3.1
#+begin_quote
A Grammian matrix $R$ is always positive-semidefinite ($x^HRx \ge 0 \forall x \in \mathbb{C}^m$). It is positive definite $\iff$ the
vectors $p_1, p_2, ..., p_n$ are linearly independent.
#+end_quote

*It is of note that if the set of vectors $\{p\}$ are orthogonal, then the Grammian becomes a diagonal matrix.*

** The orthogonality principle
#+name theorem3.2
#+begin_quote
Let $p_1, p_2, ..., p_n$ be data vectors in a vector space $S$. Let $x$ be any vector in $S$. In this representation

\begin{equation*}
x = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i + e = \hat{x} + e
\end{equation*}

the induced norm of the error vector $||e||$ is minimized when the error $e = x - \hat{x}$ is orthogonal to each of the
data vectors

\begin{equation*}
\Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, p_j \Big> \text{ for } 1,2,...,m
\end{equation*}

#+end_quote

*** Representations in infinite-dimensional space
The convergence of this infinite sum must be examined carefully. However, $T$ is an orthonormal set, then the
representation can be shown to converge.

** Error minimization via gradients
This section verifies the least squared solution using the gradient method used in optimization. Because we want to
minimize the error $e = x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i$, then we can write

\begin{equation*}
J(c) = \big<e,e\big> = \Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i \Big> = ||x||^2 - 2\text{Re}(c^Hp) + c^H Rc
\end{equation*}

Note that =Re= in $\text{Re}(c^Hp)$ indicates the real part of the value $c^Hp$. Taking the gradient of the equation above
reduces to $Rc = p$, which is what we found before.

** Matrix representation of least-squares problem
For finite-dimensional vectors (signals), matrix notation may be employed. The approximation problem can then be written
as

#+begin_quote
Determine $c$ to minimize $||e||^2_2$ in the problem $x = Ac + e = \hat{x} + e$
#+end_quote

After some mathematical manipulation we find the important solution that for the equation $Rc = p$ the optimal
(least-squares) solution is

\begin{equation*}
c = (A^H A)^{-1} A^H x = R^{-1}p
\end{equation*}

Although we could use $R^{-1}$, the problem of numerical stability arises. The pseudo-inverse of $A$ ($(A^H A)^{-1}
A^H$), is more numerically stable. Furthermore, $P = (A^H A)^{-1} A^H$ is a projection matrix. Thus, we optimally
project $x$ orthogonally down into the range of $A$ and find the best solution in that range space.

*** Weighted least-squares
This is naturally incorporated into the inner product. As we have seen before $\big< x,y \big>_W = y^H W x$, from this
we can expand and solve similarly as before to achieve the following solution.

\begin{equation*}
c = (A^H W A)^{-1} A^H W x
\end{equation*}

*** Statistical properties of the least-squares estimate
Suppose that we have a true model $x = Ac_0 + e$ that we wish to estimate. Using our solution of $c$ results in an
expected value of $c_0$. Such an estimate is said to be unbiased.

\begin{equation*}
E[c] = E[c_0 + (A^H A)^{-1} A^H e] = c_0
\end{equation*}

Furthermore, the estimator $c$ is said to be the best linear unbiased estimator. See the book for the proof.

** Minimum error in vector-space approximations
In this section we examine how much error is left when an optimal (minimal-norm) solution is obtained. Using the
Pythagorean theorem, the squared norm of the minimum error is

\begin{equation*}
||e_{min}||^2 = ||x||^2 - ||\hat{x}||^2
\end{equation*}

Using the matrix representation we can write

\begin{equation*}
||e_{min}||^2 = x^Hx - c^Hp
\end{equation*}

And using $\hat{x}  = Ac = A(A^H A)^{-1} A^H x$ we can write

\begin{equation*}
||e_{min}||^2 = x^H (I - A(A^HA)^{-1} A^H)x
\end{equation*}

** Approximation by continuous polynomials
Say we want to approximate a real continuous function $f(t)$ over an interval $t \in [a,b]$ to minimize the norm

\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b (f(t) - p(t))^2 dt
\end{equation*}

Where $p(t)$ has a degree of $m-1$. We take the basis vectors to be $\{1,t,t^2,...,t^{m-1}\}$. Using the inner product,
the principal of orthogonality, and appliying the inner product $\int_a^b f(t)g(t) dt$ we get the resulting Grammian
matrix

\begin{equation*}
R =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 1/2 & \cdots & 1/m\\
1/2 & 1/3 & \cdots & 1/(m+1)\\
\vdots          &            &    & \vdots          \\
1/m & 1/(m+1) & \cdots & 1/(2m)\\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

A matrix of this form is known as a Hilbert matrix. It becomes ill conditioned very easily as $m$ increases. Although the
Hilbert matrix is ill conditioned, we can use a set of orthogonal polynomials that span the same set which will be well
conditioned.

If certain regions are of more interest, weighted norms can be taken

\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b w(t)(f(t) - p(t))^2 dt
\end{equation*}

which induces the inner product

\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b f(t)g(t) dt
\end{equation*}

** Approximation by discrete polynomials
This section is extremely analogous to the previos section but is of more practical value. We can approximate discrete
(sampled) data similarly by using a set of discrete-time basis functions $\{1,k,...,k^{m-1}\}$. *We desire to
fit an $(m-1)$st order polynomial through the data points $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$ so that $x_k \approx p(k) \text{ for }
k=1,2,..., n$ where $p(k) = c_0 + c_1k + c_2 k^2 + ... + c_{m-1}k^{m-1}$*

If $m=n$ then there exists a polynomial that will pass through all $n$ points. If $m < n$, which is what we have an
interested in, then we want to reduce the squared error

\begin{equation*}
\sum_{k=1}^n |x_n - p(k)|^2 = || x - p ||_2^2
\end{equation*}

The first reprsentation is in summation form and the second is in vector form. This is induced from the inner product
$\big<x,y\big> = x^H y$. We are left with the equation $x = Pc + e$ This is interpreted as the data we wish to
approximate equals our approximation plus the error. This merely brings us back to our Grammian matrix, which we can use
the least-squares solution to produce

\begin{equation*}
c = (P^TP)^{-1}P^Tx
\end{equation*}

Where the transpose $\cdot^T$ can be replaced with the conjugate tranpose $\cdot^H$ if necessary. This we have our solution

\begin{equation*}
p = Pc = P(P^TP)^{-1}P^Tx
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_1 & 1 \\
x_2 & 1 \\
\vdots & \vdots \\
x_n & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

This gives rise to the equation

\begin{equation*}
y = Ac + e
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

** Least-squares filtering
The goal of least-squares filtering problem is to filter a sequence of input data $\{f[t]\}$ suing a filter with impulse
response $h[t]$ of length $m$ to produce an output that matches a desired sequence $\{d[t]\}$ as closely as possible.

Let the output of the filter be denoted as

\begin{equation*}
y[t] = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} h[i]f[t-i]
\end{equation*}

And let $d[t] = y[t] + e[t]$ as before. When doing least-squares filtering, the criterion of minimal error is that the
sum of the squared errors is as small as possible

\begin{equation*}
\text{min} \sum_{i_1}^i_2 |e[i]|^2
\end{equation*}

Where $i_1$ and $i_2$ are the starting and end points of the data indices we desire to minimize. And just as Bob is you
uncle, we get the expected solution

\begin{equation*}
h = (A^HA)^{-1}A^hd
\end{equation*}

The interesting bit here is that $A$ can be structured in a couple different ways to get different filtering techniques.

- The "covariance" method

  In this method, only the data that is explicitly given is used. There are no assumptions made about unknown data
  outside the observed data. The Grammian $A^HA$ produced by this is a Hermitian matrix.


\begin{equation*}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
f[m] &  f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\
f[m+1] &  f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[N] &  f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

- The "autocorrelation" method

  In this case, the data prior to $f[1]$ and after $f[N]$ are assumed to all be zero and are padded in the matrix as
  such. The Grammian matrix produced by this is an Toeplitz matrix.

\begin{equation*}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
f[1] &  0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
f[2] &  f[1]  & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
f[3] &  f[2] & f[1] & \cdots & 0 \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[m] &  f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\
f[m+1] &  f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[N] &  f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\
0 &  f[N] & f[N-1] & \cdots & f[N-m+2] \\
0 &  0 & 0 & \cdots & f[N] \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

- Pre-windowing method

  This method assumes that $f[t] = 0$ for $t<1$, and use data up to $f[N], so that $i_1 = 1$ and $i_2 = N$.

\begin{equation*}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
f[1] &  0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
f[2] &  f[1]  & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\
f[3] &  f[2] & f[1] & \cdots & 0 \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[m] &  f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\
f[m+1] &  f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[N] &  f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

- Post-windowing method

  This method begins with $f[t] = m$ and assumes that data after $N$ are equal to zero.

\begin{equation*}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
f[m] &  f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\
f[m+1] &  f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
f[N] &  f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\
0    &  f[N] & f[N-1] & \cdots & f[N-m+2] \\
\vdots     &      &       &    &     \\
0    &  0   & 0     & \cdots & f[N] \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

Take a look at the examples 3.9.1 through 3.9.3.

** Minimum mean-squared estimation
Up until now we have not assumed the existence of probabilistic models. In this section we change our viewpoint by
introducing probability.

Let $P_1, P_2, ..., P_m$ be zero mean random variables. We desire to find the coefficients $\{c_i\}$ to estimate the
random variable $X$ using

\begin{equation*}
X  = c_1P_1 + c_2P_2 + \cdots + c_m P_m + e
\end{equation*}

In such a way that the norm of the squared error is minimized. We now employ the inner product $\big<X,Y\big> =
E[X\bar{Y}]$ to which we find a similar looking matrix

\begin{equation*}
\begin{bmatrix}
<P_1, \bar{P_1}> & <P_2, \bar{P_1}> & \cdots & <P_M, \bar{P_1}> \\
<P_1, \bar{P_2}> & <P_2, \bar{P_2}> & \cdots & <P_M, \bar{P_2}> \\
\vdots          &            &    & \vdots          \\
<P_1, \bar{P_M}> & <P_2, \bar{P_M}> & \cdots & <P_M}, \bar{P_1}> \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}

We continue to hold the form of $Rc = p$, but now just need to find a way to solve this with the expected values inside.
The minimum mean-squared error is given by

\begin{equation*}
||e||_{min}^2 = \sigma_x^2 p^Hc
\end{equation*}

** Minimum mean-squared error (MMSE) filtering
A minimum mean-square (MMS) filter is called a Wiener filter. It is mathematically similar to a least-squares filter,
except that the expectation operator is used as the inner product. Similarly, we have data $\{f[t]\}$ and want to design
a filter in such a way that we get as close as possible to some desired sequence $d[t]$. Focusing on the FIR filter with
$m$ coefficients, the output filter can be written as

\begin{equation*}
y[t] f[t]^H h
\end{equation*}

Expressed in matrix form $Rh = p$, so the solution is $h = R^{-1}p$. Not much is said about how to actually get the $R$
matrix because Moon is an ass. The matrix $R$ is the Grammian and has a Toeplitz structure.

See example 3.11.1 for some actually juicy stuff.

** Comparison of least-squared and minimum mean-squares
For the method of least-squares the following observations are made

1. Only the sequence of data observed at the time of the estimate is used in forming the estimate.
2. Depending upon assumptions made about the data before and after the observation interval, the Grammian matrix may not
   be Toeplitz.
3. No statistical model is necessarily assumed.

For minimum mean-squares the following observations are made

1. A statistical model for the correlations and cross-correlations is necessary. This must be obtained either from
   explicit knowledge of the channel and signal (as was seen in example 3.11 .I), or on the basis of the multivariable
   distribution of the data (as was seen in example 3.10.1). In the absence of such knowledge, it is common to estimate
   the necessary autocorrelation and cross-correlation values.
2. Commonly, the coefficients of the MMS technique are computed using a separate set of data whose statistics are
   assumed to be the same as those of the real data set of interest. This set of data is used as a training set to find
   the autocorrelation functions and the filter coefficients.
3. We also note that the (true) Grammian matrix R used in prediction and optimal FIR filtering problems is always a
   Toeplitz matrix, and hence fast algorithms apply to finding the coefficients.

*** Brief review of stochastic processes and Laplace transforms
The function $S_x(j\omega)$ is the usual power spectral density function. The autocovariance is real and even, that is

$$
S_x(s) = S_x(-s)
$$

Furthermore when $s=j\omega$

$$
S_x(-\omega) = S^*_x(\omega)
$$

*** Filtering of stochatsic processes
If the input $x$ and output $y$ are wide sense stationary stochastic processes the correlation functions are given by

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{l}
R_{xy}(\tau) = R_x(\tau) \ast h(-\tau) \\
R_{yx}(\tau) = h(\tau) \ast R_x(\tau)  \\
R_y(\tau)    = h(\tau) \ast R_x(\tau) \ast h(-\tau)
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

equivalently for the spectral domain

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{l}
S_{xy}(s) = S_x(s) H(s) \\
S_{yx}(s) = H(s) R_x(s)  \\
S_y(s)    = h(s) R_x(s) H(-s)
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

*** Lumped systems and processes
A linear system is said to be lumped if it has a rational transfer function; that is, its transfer function is a ratio of polynomials in $s$. Thus a rational transfer function is of the form below. Re also require that $n \ge m$

$$
G(s) = \frac{\Pi_{i=1}^m (s-z_i)}{\Pi_{i=1}^n (s-p_i)}
$$

A stochastic process is said to be lumped if its power spectral density is a rational function.

** A dual approximation problem
Up until now we have selected a finite-dimensional subspace of the Hilbert space which involved solving an $m\times m$ system of equations. In some problems the subspace in which the solution lies is not finite dimensional. There are some problems where a finite set of constraints provides sufficient information to solve the problem from a finite set of equations.

#+begin_definition
Let $M$ be a subspace of a linear space $S$, and let $x_0 \in S$. The set $V = x_0 +M$ is said to be a translation of $M$ by $x_0$. This translation is called a linear variety. In general, a linear variety is not a subspace!
#+end_definition

Let $V = x_0 + M$ be a closed linear variety in a hilbert space. There exists a unique vector $v_0 \in V$ of minimum norm. Thie minimizing vector $v_0$ is orthogonal to $M$. Typically when we have seen problems like this the inner product $\big<x,y_i\big> = 0\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n$. The subspace defined by the set of inner products is $M^{\perp}$. Now that we have space that is offset we get non-zero results.

$$
\big<x,y_i\big> = a_i\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n
$$

Hence the space of solutions from the inner products above is the linear variety. A linear variety satisfying the $n$ constraints is said to be a codimension n, since the orthogonal compliment of the subpspace $M^\perp$ producing it has dimension $n$.

*See Example 3.14.2*

#+begin_theorem
Let ${y_i}$ be a linearly independent in a Hilbert space $S$, and let $M = \text{span}(y_1,...,y_m)$. The element $x \in S$ satisfying

$$
\big<x,y_i\big> = a_i\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n
$$

with minimum norm lies in $M$; specifically

$$
x = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i y_i
$$

where the coefficients in this linear combination satisfy

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
<y_1,y_1> & <y_2,y_1> & \cdots & <y_m,y_1> \\
<y_1,y_2> & <y_2,y_2> & \cdots & <y_m,y_2> \\
\vdots    &           &        &           \\
<y_1,y_m> & <y_2,y_m> & \cdots & <y_m,y_m> \\
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_m
\end{bmatrix} =
\begin{bmatrix}
a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_m
\end{bmatrix}
$$
#+end_theorem

** Set of complete orthogonal functions
Much of the transformaiton theory employed in signal processing is encompassed by representations in an appropriate linear vector space. The set of basis functions for the transformation is chosen so that the coefficients convey desired information about the signal. Different basis functions allow us to extract different information about the signal.

If the basis functions are orthonormal, then the minimum squared error of the series representation is

\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^m |c_i|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^m \big< x, p_i \big>^2 \le ||x||^2
\end{equation}

Which is known as /Bessel's inequality/.

When $\{p_i\}$ is a complete basis set, then the sequence $\{c_1,c_2,...\}$ completely describes $x$.

#+begin_definition
An orthonormal set $\{p_i, i = 1,2,3,...\}$ in a Hilbert space $S$ is complete if

\begin{equation*}
x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big<x,p_i \big> p_i\; \forall x \in S
\end{equation*}
#+end_definition

When $\{p_i\}$ is a complete basis set, then the sequence $\{c_i\}$ completely describes x; there is a one-to-one relationship between $x$ and $\{c_i\}$. Furthermore, completness can be further described via the following theorem

*** Theorem 3.5
A set of orthornormal functions $\{p_i, i=1,2,...\}$ is complete in an inner product space $S$ with induced norm if any of the following equivalent statements holds:

1. $x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big<x,p_i \big> p_i\; \forall x \in S$
2. For any $\epsilon > 0$ there is an $N < \infty$ such that for all $n \ge N$, $||x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big<x,p_i \big> p_i|| < \epsilon$. In other words, we can appoximate aprbitrarily close.
3. Parseval's equality holds: $||x||^2 = \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} \big< x, p_i \big>^2 \forall x \in S$
4. If $\big< x, p_i \big> \forall i$ then $x = 0$
5. There is no nonzero function $f \in S$ for which the set $\{p_i, i=1,2,...\} \cup f$ forms an orthogonal set.

*For a finite-dimensional space $S$ of dimension $m$, to have $m$ linearly independent functions $p_k, k=1,2,...,m$ is sufficient for completeness*.

We can define different transformations depending upon the set of orthonormal basis functions we choose. Since each coefficient In the transform is a projection of $x$ onto the basis function, the transform coefficient $c_i$, determines how much of $p_i$, is in $x$. If we want to look for particular features of a signal. one way is to design a set of orthogonal basis functions that have those features and compute a transform using those signals.

If $p_i$ is a complete set then there is no error in the representation and Bessel's inequality becomes an equality known as Parseval's equality.

\begin{equation}
||x||^2 = \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} |c_i|^2
\end{equation}

Equivalently written as $||x|| = ||c||$ where the norm on the left is the $L2$ norm (if $x$ is a function) and the norm on the right is the $l2$ norm.

#+begin_lemma
If $x$ and $y$ have a generalized Fourier series representation using some orthornarmal basis set $\{p_i\}$ in a Hilbert space $S$, with $x \leftrightarrow c$ and $y \leftrightarrow b$ then

\begin{equation*}
\big< x,y \big> = \big< c,b \big>
\end{equation*}
#+end_lemma

** Signals as points; digital communications
There are several sets of complete orthogonal functions that are used in common applications. We will examine a few of the more commonly-used sets.

*** Trigonometric functions
The "familar" trigonometric functions employed in Fourier series are orthogonal. They form a complete set of functions.

\begin{equation*}
p_n(t) = \frac{e^{jnt}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}
\end{equation*}

*** Orthogonal polynomials
Different sets of orthogonal polynomials are obtained by using different weighting functions, and the inner product is taken over some given interval. Some orthogonal polynomials have shown up enough that we have given them a name. Given two polynomials $f(t)$ and $g(t)$, then they are said to be orthogonal with respect to a weighting function $w(t)$ if

\begin{equation*}
\big< f,g \big>_w = 0
\end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*}
\big< f,g \big>_w = \int_a^b w(t)f(t)g(t) dt
\end{equation*}

Using the Gram-Schmidt procedure it is possible to orthogonalize any set of polynornials with respect to any inner product. In particular the set $1,t,t^2,...,t^n$ can be orthogonalized with respect to the weighted inner product. *It can be shown that the orthogonal polynomials (properly normalized) form a complete orthonormal basis for $L_ [a,b]$.

*** Families of orthogonal polynomials
A variety of types of orthogonal polynomials have been explored over the years. One of the general motivations for this is that orthogonal polynomials can be used to provide solutions to particular differential equations. Since these orthogonal polynomials form a complete orthogonal basis, they can be used to form series solutions for any boundary conditions and input function.

*** Legendre polynomials
These are not used too often, but show up from time to time. Using a weighting function of $w(t) = 1$, the first three polynomials are

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{l}
p_0(t) = 1 \\
p_1(t) = t \\
p_2(t) = t^2 - 1/3 \\
p_3(t) = t^3 - 3/5t \\
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

Using Lemma 3.2 (not written in these notes), the general polynomial is

\begin{equation*}
tp_n(t) = \frac{n+1}{2n+1}p_{n+1} + \frac{n}{2n+1}p_{n-1}(t)
\end{equation*}

*** Chebyshev polynomials
These polynomials are orthogonal with respect to the weighting function $w(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}}$ over the interval $[-1,1]$. In particular

\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}}T_r(t)T_s(t) dt =
\begin{cases}
0 & r \ne s \\
\pi & r = s = 0 \\
\frac{\pi}{2} & r = s /ne 0
\end{cases}
\end{equation*}

* Linear Operations and Matrix Inverses
The basic goals of this chapter are as follows: to understand when solutions of linear equations exist and are unique,
and to develop insight into factors that can affect the quality of the solution.

The most familiar linear operator is a matrix, appearing in a linear equation of the form

#+name: eq:transformationmatrix
\begin{equation}
Ax = b
\end{equation}

However, in the interest of getting the most "bang for the buck," we introduce more general linear operators than
matrices.

If the matrix A is "nearly not invertible," then the answers obtained by a numerical algorithm are likely to be
unreliable. Some qualitative results regarding how close a matrix is to being noninvertible are obtained by introducing
the /condition number/ of a matrix.

** Linear Operators
In autoref:eq:transformationmatrix, we can regard $A$ as a linear transformation (or operator), which maps a vector $x$
into the column space (range) of $A$.

#+begin_definition
A transformation $A: X \rightarrow Y$, where $X$ and $Y$ are vector spaces over a ring $R$, is said to be a linear if for every
$x_1, x_2 \in X$ and all scalars $\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in R$,

\begin{equation*}
A(\alpha_1 x_1 + \alpha_2 x_2) = \alpha_1 A(x_1) + \alpha_2 A(x_2)
\end{equation*}
#+end_definition

The most important example of a linear transformation, in fact the algorithmic focus of this part of the book is the
transformation $A: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m$. In this case, $A$ is the $m \times n$ matrix with the elements from $\mathbb{R}$.

*** Linear Functionals
#+begin_definition
A funcitonal $f: X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is a mapping from a vector space $X$ to a real scalar value. (More generally, the range of the
mapping could be any set of scalars.)

If $f$ has the property $f(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha f(x) + \beta f(y)$ for all real $\alpha$ and $\beta$ and all $x,y \in X$, then $f$ is said to
be a linear functional.
#+end_definition

*Inner products are functionals*. Conversely, if $X$ is a Hilbert space (complete), then /any/ continuous linear
functional can be expressed as an inner product. This in known as the Riesz representation theorem.

** Operator Norms
Let $A$ be a linear operator $A: X \rightarrow Y$. The operator norm of $A$ is then defined as

\begin{equation*}
||A||_p = \text{sup}_{x\in X \ne 0} \frac{||Ax||_p}{||x||_p} =
\text{sup}_{x\in X, ||x||_p = 1} ||Ax||_p
\end{equation*}

Where $||\cdot||_p$ is the $p$ norm defined earlier. The norm on $A$ is said to be subordinate to the norm on $X$.
Geometrically speaking, the $||A||$ subordinate norm measures the maximum extent that $A$ transforms the unit circle.
This is demonstrated in autoref:fig:opnorm.

[[http://jde27.uk/lgla/img/operator_norm_pic.jpg][http://jde27.uk/lgla/img/operator_norm_pic.jpg]]

*** Bounded Operators
#+begin_definition
If the norm of a transformation is finite, the transformation is said to be bounded.
#+end_definition

The following theorem presents a rather remarkable fact about bounded linear operators

#+begin_theorem
A linear operator $A: X \rightarrow Y$ is bounded if and only if it is continuous
#+end_theorem

The following theorem is of great utility: by showing that linear operators from finite-dimensional spaces are
continuous, we can conclude from the previous theorem that they are also bounded. Since many of the results of this
chapter rely on bounded linear operators, this theorem reassures us that matrices - operators on finite-dimensional
spaces - will work.

#+begin_theorem
Let $A: X \rightarrow Y$ be a linear operator, where $X$ and $Y$ are normed linear spaces. If $X$ is finite-dimensional, then $A$
is continuous.
#+end_theorem

*** The Neumann Expansion
The Neumann expansion provides a useful expansion for the inverse of the linear operator $(I-A)^{-1}$.

#+begin_theorem
Suppose $||\cdot||$ is a norm satisfying the submultiplicative  property $A:x \rightarrow x$ is an operator with $||A|| < 1$. Then
$(I-A)^{-1}$ exists and

\begin{equation*}
(I-A)^{-1} = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} A^i
\end{equation*}
#+end_theorem

*** Matrix Norms
The norm $||A||_{\infty}$ is the largest row sum.

\begin{equation}
||A||_{\infty} = \text{max }_{||x||_{\infty} = 1} ||Ax||_{\infty} = \text{max }_i \sum_{j} |a_{ij}|
\end{equation}

The norm $||A||_{1}$ is the largest column sum.

\begin{equation}
||A||_{1} = \text{max }_{||x||_{1} = 1} ||Ax||_{1} = \text{max }_j \sum_{j} |a_{ij}|
\end{equation}

The norm $||A||_{2}$ is more involved than the previous two. Let $\rho(A) = \text{max }_i |\lambda_i|$ where $\lambda_i$ is the
$i^{\text{th}}$ eigenvalue of matrix $A$. The $||A||_2$ ( spectral) norm is defined as

\begin{equation}
||A||_{1} = \sqrt{\rho(A^HA)}
\end{equation}

** Adjoint Operators And Transposes
Let $A:X \rightarrow Y$ be a linear operator and let $\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle: Y \times Y \rightarrow \mathbb{C}$ be an inner product. Since $Ax \in Y$, the
inner product $\langle Ax, y \rangle$ is defined and may be viewed as a scalar linear operation on $x \in X$ The elements in $Y$ are
an example of a /dual space/.

#+begin_definition
Let $A:X \rightarrow Y$ be a linear operator from the Hilbert space $X$ to the Hilbert space $Y$. The adjoint of the operator $A$,
denoted $A^*$, is the operator $A^* : Y \rightarrow X$ such that

\begin{equation*}
 \langle Ax,y \rangle = \langle x,A^* y \rangle
\end{equation*}

for all $x \in X$ and $y \in Y$. An operator $A$ is self-adjoint if $A^* = A$.
#+end_definition

It can be shown that the adjoint of a matrix is simply the conjugate transpose

\begin{equation*}
A^* = \bar{A^T} = A^H
\end{equation*}

For real matrices it simplifies to $A^* = A^T$. Furthermore, a real matrix which is self-adjoint is symmetric
(self-adjoint matrices are necessarily square). A matrix operator $A$ that satisfies $A^* A = AA^*$ is said to be
normal.

Some properties for the adjoint is

+ $(A_1 + A_2)^* = A_1^* + A_2^*$
+ $(\alpha A)^* = \bar{\alpha}A^*$
+ $(A_2 A_1)^* = A_1^* A_2^*$
+ $(A^{-1})^* = (A^*)^{-1}$
+ $(A^H)^* = (A^*)^H$ (and simply just the transpose for real matrices)

*** A Dual Optimization Problem
The adjoint allows us to express the solution to the problem of finding the minimum-norm solution to the linear operator
$Ax = y$ when there is more than one solution.

#+begin_theorem
Let $A:X \rightarrow Y$ be a bounded linear operator form the Hilbert space $X$ to the Hilbert space $Y$. The vector $x$ of
minimum norm satisfying $Ax = y$ is given by

\begin{equation*}
x = A^* z
\end{equation*}

where $z$ is any solution of $(AA^*)z = y$.
#+end_theorem

** Geometry Of Linear Equations
The solution of the matrix equation $Ax = b$ can be considered from two different perspectives to gain insight into the
solution of linear equaitons

*** The "Intersecting Lines" POV
In general, an equation in $n$ unknowns describes a hyperplane in $n$-dimensional space, and $n$ such planes must
intersect at the point of simultaneous solution. From this interpretation of intersecting lines or planes, three
different cases can be considered

+ A single solution
+ No solution
+ Infinite solutions

*** The "vector space" point of view
In general, the product of a matrix times a vector is a linear combination of the columns of the matrix. Which leads
into the following definition

#+begin_definition
The space spanned by the columns of a matrix is called the column space of the matrix. It is also called the range of
the matrix, and is denoted by $\mathcal{R}(A)$. If $A$ is written in terms of its columns, $A=\{a_1, a_2,...,a_n\}$, then

\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{R}(A) = span(a_1, a_2,...,a_n)
\end{equation*}

If $b \in \mathcal{R}(A)$ for a matrix $A$, then there must be some linear combination of the columns of $A$ that is equal to $b$.
*That is, the equation $Ax = b$ has a solution only if $b$ lies in the column space of $A$*.
#+end_definition

** Four Fundamental Subspaces Of A Linear Operator
The first two subspaces are familiar: range (column space as we have just introduced) and nullspace.

#+begin_definition
The *nullspace* of a linear operator $A:X \rightarrow Y$ consists of all the vectors $x \in X$ such that $Ax = 0$. It is denoted as
$\mathcal{N}(A)$, and is a subspace of $A$. The dimension of $\mathcal{N}(A)$ is called the nullity of $A$.
#+end_definition

The next fundamental subspace of $A$ is the *range of the adjoint ($\mathcal{R}(A^*)$)*. If $A$ is a matrix, then the adjoint is
simply the transpose. That means $\mathcal{R}(A^*)$ is simply the linear combinations of the conjugates of the rows of $A$. For
this reason, $\mathcal{R}(A^*)$ is referred to as the row space of $A^*$.

The fourth fundamental subspace is the nullspace of the adjoint ($\mathcal{N}(A^*)$). The following properties arise and are
written in the following theorem

#+begin_theorem
Let $A:X \rightarrow Y$ be a bounded linear operator between two Hilbert spaces $X$
 and $Y$, and let $\mathcal{R}(A)$ and $\mathcal{R}(A^*)$ be closed. Then

+ the range is orthogonal compliment of the left nullspace: $[\mathcal{R}(A)]^{\perp} = \mathcal{N}(A^*)$
+ the orthogonal compliment of the row space is the nullspace: $[\mathcal{R}(A)]^{\perp} = \mathcal{N}(A)$

Complementing these we get

+ $\mathcal{R}(A)= [\mathcal{N}(A^*)]^{\perp}$
+ $\mathcal{R}(A)= [\mathcal{N}(A^*)]^{\perp}$
#+end_theorem

This theorem gives us a means of determining whether an equation of the form $Ax = b$ has a solution, and whether that
solution is unique. As stated before, we know when $b$ is in the range of $A$, then there is a solution, but we can now
further state that if $b$ is in the column space of $A$, then it must also be orthogonal to the left nullspace of $A$.

#+begin_theorem
Let $A$ be a bounded linear operator. The equation $Ax = b$ has a solution if and only if $\langle b,v \rangle = 0 \forall v \in
\mathcal{N}(A^*)$. More succinctly,

\begin{equation*}
b \in \mathcal{R}(A) \iff b \perp \mathcal{N}(A^*)
\end{equation*}

In matrix notation, $Ax = b$ has a solution if and only if $v^H b = 0 \forall v : A^h v = 0$
#+end_theorem

The uniqueness of the solutions is determined by the following theorem

#+begin_theorem
The solution to the equation $Ax = b$ (if it exists) is unique if and only if the solution of $Ax = 0$ is $x=0$. That
is, if the nullspace of $A$ is the empty set (only the zero vector).
#+end_theorem

A weird little fact is that in all matrices, the dimension of the column space is the dimension of the row space (see
Example 4.5.1).

#+begin_definition
The dimension of the column space (or the row space) of the matrix $A$ is the rank of the matrix.
#+end_definition

That is we get the following relationship for a $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$:

+ Column space: $\text{dim}(\mathcal{R}(A)) = r$
+ Row space: $\text{dim}(\mathcal{R}(A^H)) = r$
+ Nullspace: $\text{dim}(\mathcal{N}(A)) = n-r$
+ Left nullspace: $\text{dim}(\mathcal{N}(A^H)) = m-r$

autoref:fig:fourfundamentalsubspaces summarizes the four fundamental subspaces. In light of the figure we observe that a
matrix $A$ maps its row space to its column space. The mapping from the row space to the column space is invertible
(although some information is never recovered due to some of the nullspace information being left behind).

#+begin_theorem
Every vector $b \in \mathcal{R}(A)$ comes form one and only one vector $x_r$ in the row space of $A$.
#+end_theorem

** Some Properties Of Matrix Inverses
#+begin_definition
A matrix $A$ is said to have a left inverse if there is a matrix $B$ such that $BA = I$, and a right inverse if there
exists a matrix $C$ such that $AC = I$.
#+end_definition

This implies that if $A$ has a right inverse, then the solution to $Ax = b$ is $x = Bb$. Similarly, for a right inverse
we get the solution $x = Cb$

*** Tests For Invertibility Of Matrices
#+begin_definition
A matrix $A \in M_{m,n}(F)$ (where $F$ is a field) is said to be nonsingular if the only $x \in F^n$ such that $Ax = 0$ is
the zero vector $x = 0$.
#+end_definition

Some other signs of Invertibility for a $n \times n$ matrix are

+ $Ax = 0$ implies $x = 0$
+ $\text{rank}(A) = n$
+ The rows of $A$ are linearly independent; the column of $A$ are linearly independent
+ The determinant of $A$ is nonzero
+ There are no zero eigenvalues of $A$
+ $A^H A$ is positive definite; that is $x^H A^H A x > 0$ for any nonzero $x \in R^n$

** Some Results On Matrix Rank
#+begin_definition
An $m \times n$ matrix is said to be full rank if the rank is as large as possible: $\text{rank}(A) = \text{min}(m,n)$. An $m
\times n$ matrix is said to be matrix deficient if it is not full rank.
#+end_definition

#+begin_theorem
For matrices $A$ and $B$ such that $AB$ exists:

+ $\mathcal{N}(B) \subset \mathcal{N}(AB)$
+ $\mathcal{R}(AB) \subset \mathcal{R}(A)$
+ $\mathcal{N}(A^*) \subset \mathcal{N}(AB)^*$
+ $\mathcal{R}(AB)^* \subset \mathcal{R}(B)$
#+end_theorem

Combining the outcome of the previous theorem we find

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{cc}
\text{rank}(AB) \le \text{rank}(A) & \text{rank}(AB) \le \text{rank}(B)
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

#+begin_theorem
Suppose $A$ is $m \times n$ and $B$ and $C$ are $n \times p$. Then $A^HAB = A^HAC$ if and only if $AB = AC$
#+end_theorem

#+begin_definition
A submatrix of a matrix $A$ is obtained by removing zero or more columns of $A$ and zero or more rows of $A$.
#+end_definition

*** The Numeric Rank
Even though the rank of a matrix is well-defined mathematically, numerical difficulties may arise when actually trying

** Another Look At Least Squares
Since we have now generalized the idea of the transpose, lets write the more general form of the least squares solution.
This follows in the given theorem

#+begin_theorem
The vector $\hat{x} \in X$ minimizes $||b - A \hat{x}||^2_2$ if and only if

\begin{equation*}
A^*A\hat{x} = A^*b
\end{equation*}
#+end_theorem

** Pseudoinverses
The solution to the theorem above $(A^HA)^{-1}A^H$ is what is called a pseudoinverse. We formally define the
pseudoinverse in a manner that captures the smallest error and the smallest norm of the solution as follows

#+begin_definition
Let $A:X \rightarrow Y$ be a bounded linear operator, where $X$ and $Y$ are Hilbert spaces, and let $\mathcal{R}(A)$ be closed. For some $b
\in Y$, let $\hat{x}$ be the vector of minimum norm $||\hat{x}||_2$ that minimizes $||b - A \hat{x}||$. The pseudoinverse
$A'$ is the operator mapping $b$ to $\ hat{x}$ for each $b \in Y$.
#+end_definition

The pseudoinverse operator takes a point in $Y$ back to a point in $\hat{x} \in \mathcal{R}(A^*)$, in such a way that $\hat{x}$ has
minimum norm. Recall that before we discussed that the inverse of the projection is possible, but some of the data is
lost due to the nullspace component. That is why we decide to go with the inverse of minimum norm.

** Matrix Condition Number
When matrices are "nearly" linearly dependent (i.e. then lines are almost parallel or are rank deficient), that may
introduce an ill-conditioned matrix. That is, a matrix that produces numerical shenanigans that we do not like or trust
the results of. Because of this, we have the concept of a matrix conditioning number that gives us an indication of how
much we can actually trust the results of using said matrix. Given $(A + \epsilon E)x = b$, we define the condition number as

\begin{equation}
\kappa(A) = \lvert A \rvert \lvert A^{-1} \rvert
\end{equation}

When $A$ is singular we take $\kappa(A) = \infty$. Now let the relative error of the perturbation $A$ be written as

\begin{equation}
\rho = \epsilon \frac{\lvert E \rvert}{\lvert A \rvert}
\end{equation}

The relative error in the solution can be bounded as follows

\begin{equation}
\frac{\lvert x_E x_0 \rvert}{\lvert x_0 \rvert} \le \kappa(A)\rho + O(\lvert \epsilon E\rvert^2)
\end{equation}

*A matrix with a large condition number is said to be ill conditioned*. Another interpretation of the condition number
can be taken by viewing the ration of the largest and smallest eigenvalues.

\begin{equation}
\kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\text{max}}}{\lambda_{\text{min}}}
\end{equation}

** Inverse Of A Small-Rank Adjustment
If we know the inverse of a matrix $A^{-1}$ is known and then $A$ is changed by the addition of some rank 1 matrix to
produce $B = A + xy^H$. We can calculate the inverse of the incremented matrix with less computation by using the matrix
inversion lemma (Sherman-Morrison formula)

\begin{equation}
B^{-1} = (A + xy^H)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1} xy^H A^{-1}}{1 + y^HA^{-1}x}
\end{equation}

*** An Application: The Recursive Least Squares (RLS) filter
Given a signal $f[t]$ and having it pass through a filter $h[t]$ to produce an output $y[t]$ such that

\begin{equation}
y[t] = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} h[i] f[t-i]
\end{equation}

The solution for $h$ is $h = R^{-1}A^Hd = R^{-1}p$. $R$ can be written as $R[t] = \sum_{i=1}^t q[i]q^H[i]$. That is we can
write $R[t] = \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} q[i]q^H[i] + q[t]q^H[t]$. Then by the Sherman-Morrison formula we can recalculate the
inverse.

** Inverse Of A Block (Partitioned) Matrix
#+begin_lemma
For a matrix $A$ partitioned as

\begin{equation}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11} & A_{12} \\
A_{21} & A_{22} \\
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

the inverse is

\begin{equation}
A =
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11} & A_{12} \\
A_{21} & A_{22} \\
\end{bmatrix}^{-1} =
\begin{bmatrix}
A_{11}^{-1} & 0     \\
0     & 0     \\
\end{bmatrix} +
\begin{bmatrix}
-A_{11}^{-1} A_{12} \\
I \\
\end{bmatrix}^{-1} S^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}
-A_{21}A_{11}^{-1} & I
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
#+end_lemma

Where $S = A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12}$.
