3. Representation and approximation in vector spaces

3.1. The approximation problem in Hilbert space

The analysis problem is this: given a vector \(x \in S\), find the coefficients \(c_1, c_2, ..., c_m\) that

\begin{equation*} \hat{x} = c_1 p_1 + c_2 p_2 + \cdots + c_m p_m \end{equation*}

Where the \(\hat{\cdot}\) is used in indicate an approximation \(x = \hat{x} + e\).

\begin{equation*} ||x - \hat{x}|| = ||e|| \end{equation*}

When using the \(l_2\) or \(L_2\) norm, this is called the least-squares problem. Because we know that the error is minimized when the error is perpendicular to the data, we can leverage the inner-product (also the \(l_2\) norm) to minimize the problem by writing

\begin{equation*} \Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, p_j \Big> \text{ for } 1,2,...,m \end{equation*}

In matrix form this equates to

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \langle p_1, p_1 \rangle & \langle p_2, p_1 \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_1 \rangle \\ \langle p_1, p_2 \rangle & \langle p_2, p_2 \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_2 \rangle \\ \vdots & & & \vdots \\ \langle p_1, p_m \rangle & \langle p_2, p_m \rangle & \cdots & \langle p_m, p_1 \rangle \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_m \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \langle x,p1 \rangle \\ \langle x,p2 \rangle \\ \vdots \\ \langle x,pm \rangle \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

3.1.1. The Grammian Matrix

Let \(R\) be called the Grammian matrix.

\begin{equation*} R = \begin{bmatrix} & & \cdots & \\ & & \cdots & \\ \vdots & & & \vdots \\ & & \cdots & \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

The Grammian is a Hermitian symmetric matrix; that is \(R^H = R\) where \(\cdot^H\) is the conjugate transpose. This also implies that \(R\) is invertible. This brings us to the following theorem

#+name theorem3.1

A Grammian matrix \(R\) is always positive-semidefinite (\(x^HRx \ge 0 \forall x \in \mathbb{C}^m\)). It is positive definite \(\iff\) the vectors \(p_1, p_2, ..., p_n\) are linearly independent.

It is of note that if the set of vectors \(\{p\}\) are orthogonal, then the Grammian becomes a diagonal matrix.

3.2. The orthogonality principle

#+name theorem3.2

Let \(p_1, p_2, ..., p_n\) be data vectors in a vector space \(S\). Let \(x\) be any vector in \(S\). In this representation

\begin{equation*} x = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i + e = \hat{x} + e \end{equation*}

the induced norm of the error vector \(||e||\) is minimized when the error \(e = x - \hat{x}\) is orthogonal to each of the data vectors

\begin{equation*} \Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, p_j \Big> \text{ for } 1,2,...,m \end{equation*}

3.2.1. Representations in infinite-dimensional space

The convergence of this infinite sum must be examined carefully. However, \(T\) is an orthonormal set, then the representation can be shown to converge.

3.3. Error minimization via gradients

This section verifies the least squared solution using the gradient method used in optimization. Because we want to minimize the error \(e = x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i\), then we can write

\begin{equation*} J(c) = \big = \Big< x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i, x - \sum_{i=1}^m c_i p_i \Big> = ||x||^2 - 2\text{Re}(c^Hp) + c^H Rc \end{equation*}

Note that Re in \(\text{Re}(c^Hp)\) indicates the real part of the value \(c^Hp\). Taking the gradient of the equation above reduces to \(Rc = p\), which is what we found before.

3.4. Matrix representation of least-squares problem

For finite-dimensional vectors (signals), matrix notation may be employed. The approximation problem can then be written as

Determine \(c\) to minimize \(||e||^2_2\) in the problem \(x = Ac + e = \hat{x} + e\)

After some mathematical manipulation we find the important solution that for the equation \(Rc = p\) the optimal (least-squares) solution is

\begin{equation*} c = (A^H A)^{-1} A^H x = R^{-1}p \end{equation*}

Although we could use \(R^{-1}\), the problem of numerical stability arises. The pseudo-inverse of \(A\) (\((A^H A)^{-1} A^H\)), is more numerically stable. Furthermore, \(P = (A^H A)^{-1} A^H\) is a projection matrix. Thus, we optimally project \(x\) orthogonally down into the range of \(A\) and find the best solution in that range space.

3.4.1. Weighted least-squares

This is naturally incorporated into the inner product. As we have seen before \(\big< x,y \big>_W = y^H W x\), from this we can expand and solve similarly as before to achieve the following solution.

\begin{equation*} c = (A^H W A)^{-1} A^H W x \end{equation*}

3.4.2. Statistical properties of the least-squares estimate

Suppose that we have a true model \(x = Ac_0 + e\) that we wish to estimate. Using our solution of \(c\) results in an expected value of \(c_0\). Such an estimate is said to be unbiased.

\begin{equation*} E[c] = E[c_0 + (A^H A)^{-1} A^H e] = c_0 \end{equation*}

Furthermore, the estimator \(c\) is said to be the best linear unbiased estimator. See the book for the proof.

3.5. Minimum error in vector-space approximations

In this section we examine how much error is left when an optimal (minimal-norm) solution is obtained. Using the Pythagorean theorem, the squared norm of the minimum error is

\begin{equation*} ||e_{min}||^2 = ||x||^2 - ||\hat{x}||^2 \end{equation*}

Using the matrix representation we can write

\begin{equation*} ||e_{min}||^2 = x^Hx - c^Hp \end{equation*}

And using \(\hat{x} = Ac = A(A^H A)^{-1} A^H x\) we can write

\begin{equation*} ||e_{min}||^2 = x^H (I - A(A^HA)^{-1} A^H)x \end{equation*}

3.6. Approximation by continuous polynomials

Say we want to approximate a real continuous function \(f(t)\) over an interval \(t \in [a,b]\) to minimize the norm

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b (f(t) - p(t))^2 dt \end{equation*}

Where \(p(t)\) has a degree of \(m-1\). We take the basis vectors to be \(\{1,t,t^2,...,t^{m-1}\}\). Using the inner product, the principal of orthogonality, and appliying the inner product \(\int_a^b f(t)g(t) dt\) we get the resulting Grammian matrix

\begin{equation*} R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & \cdots & 1/m\\ 1/2 & 1/3 & \cdots & 1/(m+1)\\ \vdots & & & \vdots \\ 1/m & 1/(m+1) & \cdots & 1/(2m)\\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

A matrix of this form is known as a Hilbert matrix. It becomes ill conditioned very easily as \(m\) increases. Although the Hilbert matrix is ill conditioned, we can use a set of orthogonal polynomials that span the same set which will be well conditioned.

If certain regions are of more interest, weighted norms can be taken

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b w(t)(f(t) - p(t))^2 dt \end{equation*}

which induces the inner product

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(t)g(t) dt \end{equation*}

3.7. Approximation by discrete polynomials

This section is extremely analogous to the previos section but is of more practical value. We can approximate discrete (sampled) data similarly by using a set of discrete-time basis functions \(\{1,k,...,k^{m-1}\}\). *We desire to fit an $(m-1)$st order polynomial through the data points \(x_1, x_2, ..., x_n\) so that \(x_k \approx p(k) \text{ for } k=1,2,..., n\) where $p(k) = c0 + c1k + c2 k2 + … + cm-1km-1$*

If \(m=n\) then there exists a polynomial that will pass through all \(n\) points. If \(m < n\), which is what we have an interested in, then we want to reduce the squared error

\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^n |x_n - p(k)|^2 = || x - p ||_2^2 \end{equation*}

The first reprsentation is in summation form and the second is in vector form. This is induced from the inner product \(\big = x^H y\). We are left with the equation \(x = Pc + e\) This is interpreted as the data we wish to approximate equals our approximation plus the error. This merely brings us back to our Grammian matrix, which we can use the least-squares solution to produce

\begin{equation*} c = (P^TP)^{-1}P^Tx \end{equation*}

Where the transpose \(\cdot^T\) can be replaced with the conjugate tranpose \(\cdot^H\) if necessary. This we have our solution

\begin{equation*} p = Pc = P(P^TP)^{-1}P^Tx \end{equation*}

Example 3.7.1 is insightful if one is desired.

3.8. Linear regression

Very similarly to before, but expanding on the concept a little. Suppose there is a set of points that we wish to fit a line over. That is, we want a function \(y_i \approx ax_i + b \text{ for } i = 1,2,...,n\). Letting \(y\) be the vector of y values, \(e\) voctor of errors, \(c = [a b]^T\), and

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & 1 \\ x_2 & 1 \\ \vdots & \vdots \\ x_n & 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

This gives rise to the equation

\begin{equation*} y = Ac + e \end{equation*}

Using the least-sqares solution, we get

\begin{equation*} c = (A^HA)^{-1}Ay \end{equation*}

3.9. Least-squares filtering

The goal of least-squares filtering problem is to filter a sequence of input data \(\{f[t]\}\) suing a filter with impulse response \(h[t]\) of length \(m\) to produce an output that matches a desired sequence \(\{d[t]\}\) as closely as possible.

Let the output of the filter be denoted as

\begin{equation*} y[t] = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} h[i]f[t-i] \end{equation*}

And let \(d[t] = y[t] + e[t]\) as before. When doing least-squares filtering, the criterion of minimal error is that the sum of the squared errors is as small as possible

\begin{equation*} \text{min} \sum_{i_1}^i_2 |e[i]|^2 \end{equation*}

Where \(i_1\) and \(i_2\) are the starting and end points of the data indices we desire to minimize. And just as Bob is you uncle, we get the expected solution

\begin{equation*} h = (A^HA)^{-1}A^hd \end{equation*}

The interesting bit here is that \(A\) can be structured in a couple different ways to get different filtering techniques.

  • The "covariance" method

    In this method, only the data that is explicitly given is used. There are no assumptions made about unknown data outside the observed data. The Grammian \(A^HA\) produced by this is a Hermitian matrix.

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} f[m] & f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\ f[m+1] & f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[N] & f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

  • The "autocorrelation" method

    In this case, the data prior to \(f[1]\) and after \(f[N]\) are assumed to all be zero and are padded in the matrix as such. The Grammian matrix produced by this is an Toeplitz matrix.

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} f[1] & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ f[2] & f[1] & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ f[3] & f[2] & f[1] & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[m] & f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\ f[m+1] & f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[N] & f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\ 0 & f[N] & f[N-1] & \cdots & f[N-m+2] \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & f[N] \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

  • Pre-windowing method

    This method assumes that \(f[t] = 0\) for \(t<1\), and use data up to $f[N], so that \(i_1 = 1\) and \(i_2 = N\).

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} f[1] & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ f[2] & f[1] & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ f[3] & f[2] & f[1] & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[m] & f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\ f[m+1] & f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[N] & f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

  • Post-windowing method

    This method begins with \(f[t] = m\) and assumes that data after \(N\) are equal to zero.

\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} f[m] & f[m-1] & f[m-2] & \cdots & f[1] \\ f[m+1] & f[m] & f[m-1] & \cdots & f[2] \\ \vdots & & & & \\ f[N] & f[N-1] & f[N-2] & \cdots & f[N-m+1] \\ 0 & f[N] & f[N-1] & \cdots & f[N-m+2] \\ \vdots & & & & \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & f[N] \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

Take a look at the examples 3.9.1 through 3.9.3.

3.9.1. Least-squares prediction and AR spectrum estimation

Consider the estimation problem where we desire to predict \(x[t]\) using a linear predictor based on \(x[t-1], x[t-2], ... x[t-m]\), As before we have a forward predictor

\begin{equation*} x[t] = -\sum_{i=1}^m a_i x[t-i] + f[t] \end{equation*}

Where \(a_i = -h_i\). We can obtain more data about the system by incorporating a backward predictor as well. Because both predictors use the same coefficients, we can use both predictors together to improve our estimate of the coefficients.

3.10. Minimum mean-squared estimation

Up until now we have not assumed the existence of probabilistic models. In this section we change our viewpoint by introducing probability.

Let \(P_1, P_2, ..., P_m\) be zero mean random variables. We desire to find the coefficients \(\{c_i\}\) to estimate the random variable \(X\) using

\begin{equation*} X = c_1P_1 + c_2P_2 + \cdots + c_m P_m + e \end{equation*}

In such a way that the norm of the squared error is minimized. We now employ the inner product \(\big = E[X\bar{Y}]\) to which we find a similar looking matrix

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} & & \cdots & \\ & & \cdots & \\ \vdots & & & \vdots \\ & & \cdots & \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

We continue to hold the form of \(Rc = p\), but now just need to find a way to solve this with the expected values inside. The minimum mean-squared error is given by

\begin{equation*} ||e||_{min}^2 = \sigma_x^2 p^Hc \end{equation*}

3.11. Minimum mean-squared error (MMSE) filtering

A minimum mean-square (MMS) filter is called a Wiener filter. It is mathematically similar to a least-squares filter, except that the expectation operator is used as the inner product. Similarly, we have data \(\{f[t]\}\) and want to design a filter in such a way that we get as close as possible to some desired sequence \(d[t]\). Focusing on the FIR filter with \(m\) coefficients, the output filter can be written as

\begin{equation*} y[t] f[t]^H h \end{equation*}

Expressed in matrix form \(Rh = p\), so the solution is \(h = R^{-1}p\). Not much is said about how to actually get the \(R\) matrix because Moon is an ass. The matrix \(R\) is the Grammian and has a Toeplitz structure.

See example 3.11.1 for some actually juicy stuff.

3.12. Comparison of least-squared and minimum mean-squares

For the method of least-squares the following observations are made

  1. Only the sequence of data observed at the time of the estimate is used in forming the estimate.
  2. Depending upon assumptions made about the data before and after the observation interval, the Grammian matrix may not be Toeplitz.
  3. No statistical model is necessarily assumed.

For minimum mean-squares the following observations are made

  1. A statistical model for the correlations and cross-correlations is necessary. This must be obtained either from explicit knowledge of the channel and signal (as was seen in example 3.11 .I), or on the basis of the multivariable distribution of the data (as was seen in example 3.10.1). In the absence of such knowledge, it is common to estimate the necessary autocorrelation and cross-correlation values.
  2. Commonly, the coefficients of the MMS technique are computed using a separate set of data whose statistics are assumed to be the same as those of the real data set of interest. This set of data is used as a training set to find the autocorrelation functions and the filter coefficients.
  3. We also note that the (true) Grammian matrix R used in prediction and optimal FIR filtering problems is always a Toeplitz matrix, and hence fast algorithms apply to finding the coefficients.

3.13.1. Brief review of stochastic processes and Laplace transforms

The function \(S_x(j\omega)\) is the usual power spectral density function. The autocovariance is real and even, that is

\[ S_x(s) = S_x(-s) \]

Furthermore when \(s=j\omega\)

\[ S_x(-\omega) = S^*_x(\omega) \]

3.13.2. Filtering of stochatsic processes

If the input \(x\) and output \(y\) are wide sense stationary stochastic processes the correlation functions are given by

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} R_{xy}(\tau) = R_x(\tau) \ast h(-\tau) \\ R_{yx}(\tau) = h(\tau) \ast R_x(\tau) \\ R_y(\tau) = h(\tau) \ast R_x(\tau) \ast h(-\tau) \end{array} \end{equation*}

equivalently for the spectral domain

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} S_{xy}(s) = S_x(s) H(s) \\ S_{yx}(s) = H(s) R_x(s) \\ S_y(s) = h(s) R_x(s) H(-s) \end{array} \end{equation*}

3.13.3. Lumped systems and processes

A linear system is said to be lumped if it has a rational transfer function; that is, its transfer function is a ratio of polynomials in \(s\). Thus a rational transfer function is of the form below. Re also require that \(n \ge m\)

\[ G(s) = \frac{\Pi_{i=1}^m (s-z_i)}{\Pi_{i=1}^n (s-p_i)} \]

A stochastic process is said to be lumped if its power spectral density is a rational function.

3.14. A dual approximation problem

Up until now we have selected a finite-dimensional subspace of the Hilbert space which involved solving an \(m\times m\) system of equations. In some problems the subspace in which the solution lies is not finite dimensional. There are some problems where a finite set of constraints provides sufficient information to solve the problem from a finite set of equations.

Let \(M\) be a subspace of a linear space \(S\), and let \(x_0 \in S\). The set \(V = x_0 +M\) is said to be a translation of \(M\) by \(x_0\). This translation is called a linear variety. In general, a linear variety is not a subspace!

Let \(V = x_0 + M\) be a closed linear variety in a hilbert space. There exists a unique vector \(v_0 \in V\) of minimum norm. Thie minimizing vector \(v_0\) is orthogonal to \(M\). Typically when we have seen problems like this the inner product \(\big = 0\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n\). The subspace defined by the set of inner products is \(M^{\perp}\). Now that we have space that is offset we get non-zero results.

\[ \big = a_i\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n \]

Hence the space of solutions from the inner products above is the linear variety. A linear variety satisfying the \(n\) constraints is said to be a codimension n, since the orthogonal compliment of the subpspace \(M^\perp\) producing it has dimension \(n\).

See Example 3.14.2

Let \({y_i}\) be a linearly independent in a Hilbert space \(S\), and let \(M = \text{span}(y_1,...,y_m)\). The element \(x \in S\) satisfying

\[ \big = a_i\; \forall i = 1,2,...,n \]

with minimum norm lies in \(M\); specifically

\[ x = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i y_i \]

where the coefficients in this linear combination satisfy

$$

\begin{bmatrix} & & \cdots & \\ & & \cdots & \\ \vdots & & & \\ & & \cdots & \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_m \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_m \end{bmatrix}

$$

3.15. Minimum-norm solution of undetermined equations

The solution to the dual problem provides a method of finding a least-squares solution to an underdetermined set of equations See Example 3.15.1. Generally speaking, given the following

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{cc} \text{Minimize} & ||x|| \\ \text{subject to} & Ax = b \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

The solution to the problem is \(x = A^H(AA^H)^{-1}b\).

3.17. Set of complete orthogonal functions

Much of the transformaiton theory employed in signal processing is encompassed by representations in an appropriate linear vector space. The set of basis functions for the transformation is chosen so that the coefficients convey desired information about the signal. Different basis functions allow us to extract different information about the signal.

If the basis functions are orthonormal, then the minimum squared error of the series representation is

\begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^m |c_i|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^m \big< x, p_i \big>^2 \le ||x||^2 \end{equation}

Which is known as Bessel's inequality.

When \(\{p_i\}\) is a complete basis set, then the sequence \(\{c_1,c_2,...\}\) completely describes \(x\).

An orthonormal set \(\{p_i, i = 1,2,3,...\}\) in a Hilbert space \(S\) is complete if

\begin{equation*} x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big p_i\; \forall x \in S \end{equation*}

When \(\{p_i\}\) is a complete basis set, then the sequence \(\{c_i\}\) completely describes x; there is a one-to-one relationship between \(x\) and \(\{c_i\}\). Furthermore, completness can be further described via the following theorem

3.17.1. Theorem 3.5

A set of orthornormal functions \(\{p_i, i=1,2,...\}\) is complete in an inner product space \(S\) with induced norm if any of the following equivalent statements holds:

  1. \(x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big p_i\; \forall x \in S\)
  2. For any \(\epsilon > 0\) there is an \(N < \infty\) such that for all \(n \ge N\), \(||x = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \big p_i|| < \epsilon\). In other words, we can appoximate aprbitrarily close.
  3. Parseval's equality holds: \(||x||^2 = \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} \big< x, p_i \big>^2 \forall x \in S\)
  4. If \(\big< x, p_i \big> \forall i\) then \(x = 0\)
  5. There is no nonzero function \(f \in S\) for which the set \(\{p_i, i=1,2,...\} \cup f\) forms an orthogonal set.

For a finite-dimensional space \(S\) of dimension \(m\), to have \(m\) linearly independent functions \(p_k, k=1,2,...,m\) is sufficient for completeness.

We can define different transformations depending upon the set of orthonormal basis functions we choose. Since each coefficient In the transform is a projection of \(x\) onto the basis function, the transform coefficient \(c_i\), determines how much of \(p_i\), is in \(x\). If we want to look for particular features of a signal. one way is to design a set of orthogonal basis functions that have those features and compute a transform using those signals.

If \(p_i\) is a complete set then there is no error in the representation and Bessel's inequality becomes an equality known as Parseval's equality.

\begin{equation} ||x||^2 = \sum_{x=1}^{\infty} |c_i|^2 \end{equation}

Equivalently written as \(||x|| = ||c||\) where the norm on the left is the \(L2\) norm (if \(x\) is a function) and the norm on the right is the \(l2\) norm.

If \(x\) and \(y\) have a generalized Fourier series representation using some orthornarmal basis set \(\{p_i\}\) in a Hilbert space \(S\), with \(x \leftrightarrow c\) and \(y \leftrightarrow b\) then

\begin{equation*} \big< x,y \big> = \big< c,b \big> \end{equation*}

3.18. Signals as points; digital communications

There are several sets of complete orthogonal functions that are used in common applications. We will examine a few of the more commonly-used sets.

3.18.1. Trigonometric functions

The "familar" trigonometric functions employed in Fourier series are orthogonal. They form a complete set of functions.

\begin{equation*} p_n(t) = \frac{e^{jnt}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \end{equation*}

3.18.2. Orthogonal polynomials

Different sets of orthogonal polynomials are obtained by using different weighting functions, and the inner product is taken over some given interval. Some orthogonal polynomials have shown up enough that we have given them a name. Given two polynomials \(f(t)\) and \(g(t)\), then they are said to be orthogonal with respect to a weighting function \(w(t)\) if

\begin{equation*} \big< f,g \big>_w = 0 \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} \big< f,g \big>_w = \int_a^b w(t)f(t)g(t) dt \end{equation*}

Using the Gram-Schmidt procedure it is possible to orthogonalize any set of polynornials with respect to any inner product. In particular the set \(1,t,t^2,...,t^n\) can be orthogonalized with respect to the weighted inner product. *It can be shown that the orthogonal polynomials (properly normalized) form a complete orthonormal basis for \(L_ [a,b]\).

3.18.3. Families of orthogonal polynomials

A variety of types of orthogonal polynomials have been explored over the years. One of the general motivations for this is that orthogonal polynomials can be used to provide solutions to particular differential equations. Since these orthogonal polynomials form a complete orthogonal basis, they can be used to form series solutions for any boundary conditions and input function.

3.18.4. Legendre polynomials

These are not used too often, but show up from time to time. Using a weighting function of \(w(t) = 1\), the first three polynomials are

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} p_0(t) = 1 \\ p_1(t) = t \\ p_2(t) = t^2 - 1/3 \\ p_3(t) = t^3 - 3/5t \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

Using Lemma 3.2 (not written in these notes), the general polynomial is

\begin{equation*} tp_n(t) = \frac{n+1}{2n+1}p_{n+1} + \frac{n}{2n+1}p_{n-1}(t) \end{equation*}

3.18.5. Chebyshev polynomials

These polynomials are orthogonal with respect to the weighting function \(w(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}}\) over the interval \([-1,1]\). In particular

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}}T_r(t)T_s(t) dt = \begin{cases} 0 & r \ne s \\ \pi & r = s = 0 \\ \frac{\pi}{2} & r = s /ne 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

4. Linear Operations and Matrix Inverses

The basic goals of this chapter are as follows: to understand when solutions of linear equations exist and are unique, and to develop insight into factors that can affect the quality of the solution.

The most familiar linear operator is a matrix, appearing in a linear equation of the form

\begin{equation} \label{org2b9fe59} Ax = b \end{equation}

However, in the interest of getting the most "bang for the buck," we introduce more general linear operators than matrices.

If the matrix A is "nearly not invertible," then the answers obtained by a numerical algorithm are likely to be unreliable. Some qualitative results regarding how close a matrix is to being noninvertible are obtained by introducing the condition number of a matrix.

4.1. Linear Operators

In eq:transformationmatrix, we can regard \(A\) as a linear transformation (or operator), which maps a vector \(x\) into the column space (range) of \(A\).

A transformation \(A: X \rightarrow Y\), where \(X\) and \(Y\) are vector spaces over a ring \(R\), is said to be a linear if for every \(x_1, x_2 \in X\) and all scalars \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \in R\),

\begin{equation*} A(\alpha_1 x_1 + \alpha_2 x_2) = \alpha_1 A(x_1) + \alpha_2 A(x_2) \end{equation*}

The most important example of a linear transformation, in fact the algorithmic focus of this part of the book is the transformation \(A: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m\). In this case, \(A\) is the \(m \times n\) matrix with the elements from \(\mathbb{R}\).

4.1.1. Linear Functionals

A funcitonal \(f: X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is a mapping from a vector space \(X\) to a real scalar value. (More generally, the range of the mapping could be any set of scalars.)

If \(f\) has the property \(f(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha f(x) + \beta f(y)\) for all real \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) and all \(x,y \in X\), then \(f\) is said to be a linear functional.

Inner products are functionals. Conversely, if \(X\) is a Hilbert space (complete), then any continuous linear functional can be expressed as an inner product. This in known as the Riesz representation theorem.

4.2. Operator Norms

Let \(A\) be a linear operator \(A: X \rightarrow Y\). The operator norm of \(A\) is then defined as

\begin{equation*} ||A||_p = \text{sup}_{x\in X \ne 0} \frac{||Ax||_p}{||x||_p} = \text{sup}_{x\in X, ||x||_p = 1} ||Ax||_p \end{equation*}

Where \(||\cdot||_p\) is the \(p\) norm defined earlier. The norm on \(A\) is said to be subordinate to the norm on \(X\). Geometrically speaking, the \(||A||\) subordinate norm measures the maximum extent that \(A\) transforms the unit circle. This is demonstrated in fig:opnorm.

4.2.1. Bounded Operators

If the norm of a transformation is finite, the transformation is said to be bounded.

The following theorem presents a rather remarkable fact about bounded linear operators

A linear operator \(A: X \rightarrow Y\) is bounded if and only if it is continuous

The following theorem is of great utility: by showing that linear operators from finite-dimensional spaces are continuous, we can conclude from the previous theorem that they are also bounded. Since many of the results of this chapter rely on bounded linear operators, this theorem reassures us that matrices - operators on finite-dimensional spaces - will work.

Let \(A: X \rightarrow Y\) be a linear operator, where \(X\) and \(Y\) are normed linear spaces. If \(X\) is finite-dimensional, then \(A\) is continuous.

4.2.2. The Neumann Expansion

The Neumann expansion provides a useful expansion for the inverse of the linear operator \((I-A)^{-1}\).

Suppose \(||\cdot||\) is a norm satisfying the submultiplicative property \(A:x \rightarrow x\) is an operator with \(||A|| < 1\). Then \((I-A)^{-1}\) exists and

\begin{equation*} (I-A)^{-1} = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} A^i \end{equation*}

4.2.3. Matrix Norms

The norm \(||A||_{\infty}\) is the largest row sum.

\begin{equation} ||A||_{\infty} = \text{max }_{||x||_{\infty} = 1} ||Ax||_{\infty} = \text{max }_i \sum_{j} |a_{ij}| \end{equation}

The norm \(||A||_{1}\) is the largest column sum.

\begin{equation} ||A||_{1} = \text{max }_{||x||_{1} = 1} ||Ax||_{1} = \text{max }_j \sum_{j} |a_{ij}| \end{equation}

The norm \(||A||_{2}\) is more involved than the previous two. Let \(\rho(A) = \text{max }_i |\lambda_i|\) where \(\lambda_i\) is the \(i^{\text{th}}\) eigenvalue of matrix \(A\). The \(||A||_2\) ( spectral) norm is defined as

\begin{equation} ||A||_{1} = \sqrt{\rho(A^HA)} \end{equation}

4.3. Adjoint Operators And Transposes

Let \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) be a linear operator and let \(\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle: Y \times Y \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\) be an inner product. Since \(Ax \in Y\), the inner product \(\langle Ax, y \rangle\) is defined and may be viewed as a scalar linear operation on \(x \in X\) The elements in \(Y\) are an example of a dual space.

Let \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) be a linear operator from the Hilbert space \(X\) to the Hilbert space \(Y\). The adjoint of the operator \(A\), denoted \(A^*\), is the operator \(A^* : Y \rightarrow X\) such that

\begin{equation*} \langle Ax,y \rangle = \langle x,A^* y \rangle \end{equation*}

for all \(x \in X\) and \(y \in Y\). An operator \(A\) is self-adjoint if \(A^* = A\).

It can be shown that the adjoint of a matrix is simply the conjugate transpose

\begin{equation*} A^* = \bar{A^T} = A^H \end{equation*}

For real matrices it simplifies to \(A^* = A^T\). Furthermore, a real matrix which is self-adjoint is symmetric (self-adjoint matrices are necessarily square). A matrix operator \(A\) that satisfies \(A^* A = AA^*\) is said to be normal.

Some properties for the adjoint is

  • \((A_1 + A_2)^* = A_1^* + A_2^*\)
  • \((\alpha A)^* = \bar{\alpha}A^*\)
  • \((A_2 A_1)^* = A_1^* A_2^*\)
  • \((A^{-1})^* = (A^*)^{-1}\)
  • \((A^H)^* = (A^*)^H\) (and simply just the transpose for real matrices)

4.3.1. A Dual Optimization Problem

The adjoint allows us to express the solution to the problem of finding the minimum-norm solution to the linear operator \(Ax = y\) when there is more than one solution.

Let \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) be a bounded linear operator form the Hilbert space \(X\) to the Hilbert space \(Y\). The vector \(x\) of minimum norm satisfying \(Ax = y\) is given by

\begin{equation*} x = A^* z \end{equation*}

where \(z\) is any solution of \((AA^*)z = y\).

4.4. Geometry Of Linear Equations

The solution of the matrix equation \(Ax = b\) can be considered from two different perspectives to gain insight into the solution of linear equaitons

4.4.1. The "Intersecting Lines" POV

In general, an equation in \(n\) unknowns describes a hyperplane in $n$-dimensional space, and \(n\) such planes must intersect at the point of simultaneous solution. From this interpretation of intersecting lines or planes, three different cases can be considered

  • A single solution
  • No solution
  • Infinite solutions

4.4.2. The "vector space" point of view

In general, the product of a matrix times a vector is a linear combination of the columns of the matrix. Which leads into the following definition

The space spanned by the columns of a matrix is called the column space of the matrix. It is also called the range of the matrix, and is denoted by \(\mathcal{R}(A)\). If \(A\) is written in terms of its columns, \(A=\{a_1, a_2,...,a_n\}\), then

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{R}(A) = span(a_1, a_2,...,a_n) \end{equation*}

If \(b \in \mathcal{R}(A)\) for a matrix \(A\), then there must be some linear combination of the columns of \(A\) that is equal to \(b\). That is, the equation \(Ax = b\) has a solution only if \(b\) lies in the column space of \(A\).

4.5. Four Fundamental Subspaces Of A Linear Operator

The first two subspaces are familiar: range (column space as we have just introduced) and nullspace.

The nullspace of a linear operator \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) consists of all the vectors \(x \in X\) such that \(Ax = 0\). It is denoted as \(\mathcal{N}(A)\), and is a subspace of \(A\). The dimension of \(\mathcal{N}(A)\) is called the nullity of \(A\).

The next fundamental subspace of \(A\) is the range of the adjoint ($\mathcal{R}(A^)$)*. If \(A\) is a matrix, then the adjoint is simply the transpose. That means \(\mathcal{R}(A^*)\) is simply the linear combinations of the conjugates of the rows of \(A\). For this reason, \(\mathcal{R}(A^*)\) is referred to as the row space of \(A^*\).

The fourth fundamental subspace is the nullspace of the adjoint (\(\mathcal{N}(A^*)\)). The following properties arise and are written in the following theorem

Let \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) be a bounded linear operator between two Hilbert spaces \(X\) and \(Y\), and let \(\mathcal{R}(A)\) and \(\mathcal{R}(A^*)\) be closed. Then

  • the range is orthogonal compliment of the left nullspace: \([\mathcal{R}(A)]^{\perp} = \mathcal{N}(A^*)\)
  • the orthogonal compliment of the row space is the nullspace: \([\mathcal{R}(A)]^{\perp} = \mathcal{N}(A)\)

Complementing these we get

  • \(\mathcal{R}(A)= [\mathcal{N}(A^*)]^{\perp}\)
  • \(\mathcal{R}(A)= [\mathcal{N}(A^*)]^{\perp}\)

This theorem gives us a means of determining whether an equation of the form \(Ax = b\) has a solution, and whether that solution is unique. As stated before, we know when \(b\) is in the range of \(A\), then there is a solution, but we can now further state that if \(b\) is in the column space of \(A\), then it must also be orthogonal to the left nullspace of \(A\).

Let \(A\) be a bounded linear operator. The equation \(Ax = b\) has a solution if and only if \(\langle b,v \rangle = 0 \forall v \in \mathcal{N}(A^*)\). More succinctly,

\begin{equation*} b \in \mathcal{R}(A) \iff b \perp \mathcal{N}(A^*) \end{equation*}

In matrix notation, \(Ax = b\) has a solution if and only if \(v^H b = 0 \forall v : A^h v = 0\)

The uniqueness of the solutions is determined by the following theorem

The solution to the equation \(Ax = b\) (if it exists) is unique if and only if the solution of \(Ax = 0\) is \(x=0\). That is, if the nullspace of \(A\) is the empty set (only the zero vector).

A weird little fact is that in all matrices, the dimension of the column space is the dimension of the row space (see Example 4.5.1).

The dimension of the column space (or the row space) of the matrix \(A\) is the rank of the matrix.

That is we get the following relationship for a \(m \times n\) matrix of rank \(r\):

  • Column space: \(\text{dim}(\mathcal{R}(A)) = r\)
  • Row space: \(\text{dim}(\mathcal{R}(A^H)) = r\)
  • Nullspace: \(\text{dim}(\mathcal{N}(A)) = n-r\)
  • Left nullspace: \(\text{dim}(\mathcal{N}(A^H)) = m-r\)

fig:fourfundamentalsubspaces summarizes the four fundamental subspaces. In light of the figure we observe that a matrix \(A\) maps its row space to its column space. The mapping from the row space to the column space is invertible (although some information is never recovered due to some of the nullspace information being left behind).

Every vector \(b \in \mathcal{R}(A)\) comes form one and only one vector \(x_r\) in the row space of \(A\).

4.6. Some Properties Of Matrix Inverses

A matrix \(A\) is said to have a left inverse if there is a matrix \(B\) such that \(BA = I\), and a right inverse if there exists a matrix \(C\) such that \(AC = I\).

This implies that if \(A\) has a right inverse, then the solution to \(Ax = b\) is \(x = Bb\). Similarly, for a right inverse we get the solution \(x = Cb\)

4.6.1. Tests For Invertibility Of Matrices

A matrix \(A \in M_{m,n}(F)\) (where \(F\) is a field) is said to be nonsingular if the only \(x \in F^n\) such that \(Ax = 0\) is the zero vector \(x = 0\).

Some other signs of Invertibility for a \(n \times n\) matrix are

  • \(Ax = 0\) implies \(x = 0\)
  • \(\text{rank}(A) = n\)
  • The rows of \(A\) are linearly independent; the column of \(A\) are linearly independent
  • The determinant of \(A\) is nonzero
  • There are no zero eigenvalues of \(A\)
  • \(A^H A\) is positive definite; that is \(x^H A^H A x > 0\) for any nonzero \(x \in R^n\)

4.7. Some Results On Matrix Rank

An \(m \times n\) matrix is said to be full rank if the rank is as large as possible: \(\text{rank}(A) = \text{min}(m,n)\). An \(m \times n\) matrix is said to be matrix deficient if it is not full rank.

For matrices \(A\) and \(B\) such that \(AB\) exists:

  • \(\mathcal{N}(B) \subset \mathcal{N}(AB)\)
  • \(\mathcal{R}(AB) \subset \mathcal{R}(A)\)
  • \(\mathcal{N}(A^*) \subset \mathcal{N}(AB)^*\)
  • \(\mathcal{R}(AB)^* \subset \mathcal{R}(B)\)

Combining the outcome of the previous theorem we find

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{cc} \text{rank}(AB) \le \text{rank}(A) & \text{rank}(AB) \le \text{rank}(B) \end{array} \end{equation*}

Suppose \(A\) is \(m \times n\) and \(B\) and \(C\) are \(n \times p\). Then \(A^HAB = A^HAC\) if and only if \(AB = AC\)

A submatrix of a matrix \(A\) is obtained by removing zero or more columns of \(A\) and zero or more rows of \(A\).

4.7.1. The Numeric Rank

Even though the rank of a matrix is well-defined mathematically, numerical difficulties may arise when actually trying to compute the rank of a matrix with real-valued elements, due to roundoff.

Since we have now generalized the idea of the transpose, lets write the more general form of the least squares solution. This follows in the given theorem

4.9. Pseudoinverses

The solution to the theorem above \((A^HA)^{-1}A^H\) is what is called a pseudoinverse. We formally define the pseudoinverse in a manner that captures the smallest error and the smallest norm of the solution as follows

Let \(A:X \rightarrow Y\) be a bounded linear operator, where \(X\) and \(Y\) are Hilbert spaces, and let \(\mathcal{R}(A)\) be closed. For some \(b \in Y\), let \(\hat{x}\) be the vector of minimum norm \(||\hat{x}||_2\) that minimizes \(||b - A \hat{x}||\). The pseudoinverse \(A'\) is the operator mapping \(b\) to \(\ hat{x}\) for each \(b \in Y\).

The pseudoinverse operator takes a point in \(Y\) back to a point in \(\hat{x} \in \mathcal{R}(A^*)\), in such a way that \(\hat{x}\) has minimum norm. Recall that before we discussed that the inverse of the projection is possible, but some of the data is lost due to the nullspace component. That is why we decide to go with the inverse of minimum norm.

4.10. Matrix Condition Number

When matrices are "nearly" linearly dependent (i.e. then lines are almost parallel or are rank deficient), that may introduce an ill-conditioned matrix. That is, a matrix that produces numerical shenanigans that we do not like or trust the results of. Because of this, we have the concept of a matrix conditioning number that gives us an indication of how much we can actually trust the results of using said matrix. Given \((A + \epsilon E)x = b\), we define the condition number as

\begin{equation} \kappa(A) = \lvert A \rvert \lvert A^{-1} \rvert \end{equation}

When \(A\) is singular we take \(\kappa(A) = \infty\). Now let the relative error of the perturbation \(A\) be written as

\begin{equation} \rho = \epsilon \frac{\lvert E \rvert}{\lvert A \rvert} \end{equation}

The relative error in the solution can be bounded as follows

\begin{equation} \frac{\lvert x_E x_0 \rvert}{\lvert x_0 \rvert} \le \kappa(A)\rho + O(\lvert \epsilon E\rvert^2) \end{equation}

A matrix with a large condition number is said to be ill conditioned. Another interpretation of the condition number can be taken by viewing the ration of the largest and smallest eigenvalues.

\begin{equation} \kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\text{max}}}{\lambda_{\text{min}}} \end{equation}

4.11. Inverse Of A Small-Rank Adjustment

If we know the inverse of a matrix \(A^{-1}\) is known and then \(A\) is changed by the addition of some rank 1 matrix to produce \(B = A + xy^H\). We can calculate the inverse of the incremented matrix with less computation by using the matrix inversion lemma (Sherman-Morrison formula)

\begin{equation} B^{-1} = (A + xy^H)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1} xy^H A^{-1}}{1 + y^HA^{-1}x} \end{equation}

4.11.1. An Application: The Recursive Least Squares (RLS) filter

Given a signal \(f[t]\) and having it pass through a filter \(h[t]\) to produce an output \(y[t]\) such that

\begin{equation} y[t] = \sum_{i=0}^{m-1} h[i] f[t-i] \end{equation}

The solution for \(h\) is \(h = R^{-1}A^Hd = R^{-1}p\). \(R\) can be written as \(R[t] = \sum_{i=1}^t q[i]q^H[i]\). That is we can write \(R[t] = \sum_{i=1}^{t-1} q[i]q^H[i] + q[t]q^H[t]\). Then by the Sherman-Morrison formula we can recalculate the inverse.

4.12. Inverse Of A Block (Partitioned) Matrix

For a matrix \(A\) partitioned as

\begin{equation} A = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

the inverse is

\begin{equation} A = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \\ \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} A_{11}^{-1} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -A_{11}^{-1} A_{12} \\ I \\ \end{bmatrix}^{-1} S^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} -A_{21}A_{11}^{-1} & I \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

Where \(S = A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12}\).

5. Some Important Matrix Factorizations

  • \(LU\): \(A= LU\) where \(L\) is a lower-triangular matrix and \(U\) is upper-diagonal. Its main application is in \(Ax = b\).
  • Cholesky: \(A = LL^H\) where \(A\) is a Hermitian (symmetric) positive definite matrix.
  • \(QR\): \(A = QR\) where \(A\) is a general matrix, \(Q\) is a unitary matrix and \(R\) is upper-triangular.

5.1. The \(LU\) Factorization

In many we are faced with the equation

\begin{equation} \label{orge919def} Ax = b \end{equation}

where \(A\) has a special structure such as Toeplitz, Vandermonde, or Hankel matrix. In these instances it is advantageous to utilize \(LR\) factorization. In the special case where \(A\) is Hermitian and positive definite, the Cholesky factorization is preferred.

The \(LU\) factorization for a \(m \times m\) matrix is

\begin{equation} PA = LU \end{equation}

where \(P\) is a permutation matrix, \(L\) is a lower-diagonal and \(U\) is an upper-diagonal matrix. Using \(LU\) decomposition, eq:lubase can be decomposed into the following set of equations

\begin{equation} \begin{array}{l} LUx = Pb \\ Ux = y \end{array} \end{equation}

That is, we can solve for the \(y\) variables via forward substitution using eq:luforward

\begin{equation} \label{org3b87252} y_j = c_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1} l_{ji}y_i \end{equation}

If this is confusing, look at the structure of a diagonal matrix and make the realization that \(y_1 = l_{11}\) which can then be propagated downward. Once the \(y\) value have been found, we can then use them to find \(x\) in

\begin{equation} Ux = y \end{equation}

where the equation can be written out as eq:lubackward

\begin{equation} \label{org0e59178} x_j = \frac{1}{u{jj}} \Big( y_j - \sum_{j=j+1}^{m} u_{jk}x_k \Big) \end{equation}

The best thing about the solution is that it has better numerical properties than explicitly taking the inverse of a matrix and multiplying. For this reason it is a good rule of thumb to never explicitly invert a matrix numerically.

5.1.1. Computing the determinant using \(LU\) factorization

\begin{equation} \text{det}(A) = \text{det}(P^TLU) = \text{det}(P)\text{det}(L)\text{det}(U) = \pm \Pi_ {i=1}^m u_{ii} \end{equation}

5.1.2. Computing the \(LU\) factorization

The \(LU\) decomposition can be found be taking the Gaussian elimination of the matrix \(A\). We find the observation that

\begin{equation} A = LU = E_1^{-1}E_2^{-1}E_3^{-1}U \end{equation}

where \(E_i\) is a matrix of linear combinations of the Gaussian elimination to produce \(U\). That is to say, the sequence of inverse Gaussian manipulations to produce \(U\) is \(L\).

  1. Pivoting

    It is possible, even a well conditioned matrix, for the procedure just described to be numerically poor. *The solution to the problem (as well as the division be zero problem that could occur) is found by changing the order of the rows so that the pivot of the unreduced part of the kth column is the largest in magnitude*. That is to say, the \(a_{11}\) element will be the largest magnitude, then the Gaussian elimination will occur to remove the elements below it. Then \(a_{22}\) will be made the largest in magnitude before Gaussian elimination. This occurs for the whole matrix.

5.2. The Cholesky factorization

The Cholesky factorization is used to compute the "square root" of a positive definite \(m \times m\) Hermitian matrix as

\begin{equation} B = LL^H \end{equation}

where \(L\) is lower triangular. Sometimes the matrix \(L\) is normalized with a diagonal scalar matrix to "bring back" the original values. This is represented as

\begin{equation} B = U^H D U \end{equation}

Where \(U\) is the diagonal-normalized version of \(L\) and \(D\) is the diagonal matrix with the magnitudes of \(L\).

Cholesky is good for generating Gaussian random vectors with covariance \(R\). Systems typically have methods of producing \(N(0,1)\) random variables. We can leverage those to generate \(N(0,R)\) by writing

\begin{equation*} R = LL^T \end{equation*}

For each random vector create a vector \(x\) of \(N(0,!)\) independent random variables using the Gaussian random number generator and let

\begin{equation*} z = Lx \end{equation*}

\(z\) will have the desired covariance.

For the equation

\begin{equation*} Ax = b \end{equation*}

Similar to before we can write

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} A = LL^H \\ Ly = b \\ L^Hx = y \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

Particularly in the least squares scenario where we have

\begin{equation*} (A^HA)x = A^H b \end{equation*}

we can let \(A^HA = LL^H\) and \(A^Hb=p\) we can solve for \(x\) via

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{l} Ly = p \\ L^Hx = y \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

5.3. Unitary matrices and the \(QR\) factorization

5.3.1. Unitary matrices

An \(m \times m\) matrix \(Q\) with complex elements is said to be unitary if

\begin{equation*} Q^H Q = I \end{equation*}

if \(Q\) has real elements and \(Q^T Q=I\), then \(Q\) is said to be an orthogonal matrix.

if \(y= Qx\) for an \(m \times m\) matrix \(Q\), then: \(||y|| = ||x|| \forall x \in \mathbb{R}\) if and only if \(Q\) is unitary, where the norm is the usual Euclidean norm.

That is to say a unitary matrix is said to be isometric transformation, or "length preserving".

if \(Y = QX\) for an \(m \times m\) unitary matrix \(Q\), then

\begin{equation*} ||Y||_F = ||X||_F \end{equation*}

where \(||\cdot||_F\) is the Frobenius norm.

5.3.2. The \(QR\) factorization

An \(m \times n\) matrix \(A\) can be written as eq:qrfact, with \(Q\) being a unitary \(m \times m\) matrix and \(R\) being an \(m \times n\) upper triangular matrix.

\begin{equation*} \label{org9ca4059} A = QR \end{equation*}

The most important application of the \(QR\) factorization is to full-rank least-squares problems. Solving the way we have before we get \(\hat{x} = (A^HA)^{-1}A^Hb\). Noting that \(A^HA\) essentially squares the condition number (not good) we can mitigate that with \(QR\) factorization. That is, write

\begin{equation*} A = QR = Q \begin{bmatrix} R_1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

where the solution is

\begin{equation*} R_1 \hat{x} = c \end{equation*}

Because we stipulated that \(R_1\) be upper-triangular, we can use back substitution as we have before.

5.3.3. \(QR\) factorization and least-squares filters

Elaborating on before we can say (in a more least-square way), given \(d[k] = A[k]h[k] + e[k]\) we can get a solution via

\begin{equation*} R_1[k]h = (Q[k]d[k]) \end{equation*}

5.3.4. Computing the \(QR\) factorization

There are at least four major ways of computing the \(QR\) factorization:

  • The Gram-Schmidt process
  • The modified Gram-Schmidt process
  • The Householder transformations

  • Givens rotations

    The Gram-Schmidt process is the easiest but produces the least numerically conditioned results.

5.3.5. Householder transformations

The idea behind Householder transformations is given a vector \(x\), find a reflection of a projection of \(x\) across a space perpendicular in such a way that \(m-i\) elements in the vector are zeroed out. This method preserves the energy of the vector into an element while zeroing out the others.

To do such a task, we need to find an appropriate vector \(v\) (Householder vector) that will help up find a transformation matrix \(H_v\) such that the following equation holds

\begin{equation} H_v = I - 2 \frac{vv^H}{v^Hv} \end{equation}

But how should \(v\) be determined? That is given by

\begin{equation} v = x \pm ||x||_2 e_1 \end{equation}

where the sign of is taken to be opposite of the direction that will take \(x\) "closer" to a multiple of \(e_1\). See the image below for a visualization.

To expand this to reduce subsequent vectors in a matrix we see that

\begin{equation} H_1A = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

Then to reduce the second column write

\begin{equation} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & H_1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ 0 & x & x \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & x & x \\ 0 & a_2 & x \\ 0 & 0 & x \\ 0 & 0 & x \\ \end{bmatrix} = Q_2 Q_1 A \end{equation}

5.3.7. \(QR\) factorization using Givens rotations

Unlike the Householder transformations which zeros out the entire column in one stroke, Givens rotations more selectively zeros out elements one at a time using rotations. We all knew ye' old rotation matrix

\begin{equation} \begin{bmatrix} cos(\theta) & sin(\theta) \\ -sin(\theta) & cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}

For a vector \(x = [x y]^T\), its second coordinate can be zeroed by multiplication by the orthogonal matrix \(G_{\theta}\), where

\begin{equation} \theta(x,y) = -tan^_{-1}\frac{y}{x} \end{equation}
