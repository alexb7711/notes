1.2.3. Decisions and Stages

  • Definitions
    • Stochastic linear programs are linear programs in which some problem data may be considered uncertain.
    • Recourse programs are those in which some decisions or recourse actions may be taken after uncertainty is disclosed.

The set of decisions is then divided into two groups:

  • A number of decisions have to be taken before the experiment. All these decisions are called first-stage decisions and the period when these decisions are taken is called the first stage.
  • A number of decisions can be taken after the experiment. They are called second-stage decisions. The corresponding period is called the second stage.

The first-stage decisions are represented by the vector \(x\) and the second-stage decision variables are represented by the vector \(y\), \(y(\omega)\), or even \(y(\omega , x)\) if one wishes to be explicit that the second-stage decisions differ as a function of the outcome of the random experiment of the first-stage. \(\omega \in \Omega\) refers to a random event.

1.2.4. Two-Stage Program with Fixed Recourse

\begin{equation} \begin{array}{c} \text{min } z = c^Tx + E_{\xi} [\text{min } q(\omega) y(\omega)] \\ \text{s.t } Ax = b \\ T(\omega)x + Wy(\omega) = h(\omega) \\ x \ge 0, y(\omega) \ge 0 \\ \end{array} \end{equation}

Consider 1. Refer to the location problem on page 61 in \cite{Birge-2011} for the location problem. This example is used is subsequent sections to demonstrate different examples of recourse formulations.

  1. Fixed distribution pattern, fixed demand, \(r_i\), \(v_j\), \(t_{ij}\) stochastic
    • Uncertainties: production and distribution costs and prices charged to the clients
    • The first stage determines the plant locations and the distribution pattern (which plant \(j\) will distribute to customer \(i\))
    • The second stage serves as a measure of the cost distribution
    • The optimal solution is then to act on the expected value of \(q_{ij}\)
  2. Fixed distribution pattern, uncertain demand
    • Demand is uncertain but the distribution plan is fixed on the first stage
    • Because the demand is uncertain we add two extra varables that will punish if too much or not enough is shipped
  3. Uncertain demand, variable distribution pattern
    • This scenairo now allows the distribution pattern to be adjusted to the realization of the random event
    • It seems that when an item is made stochastic, it is broken down into a more fundamental form that creates a cost per unit of something
      • That makes sense in the way that since are allowing it to change, we introduce the mechanism in which to change the original variable
      • In other words introduce the "differential form"
    • For the case of making the distribution pattern variable, \(y_{ij}{\omega}\). This means that the fraction of demand \(y\), is delivered by \(j\) to \(i\). (4.18)
    • The demand taken by one distribution center cannot be more than its capacity (4.21)
    • The problem is now
      • First stage: Warehouse location
      • Second stage: distribution problem
  4. Stages versus periods; Two-stage versus multistage

    Mulistage models become necessary if the original decision is expected to be re-evaluated. In the case of the previous example, a three-stage model could be useful if the firm allowed for new warehouses to be opened after a year. The first two stages would be the same as before including builing a new warehouse, and the third stage would be to get the final distribution pattern.

1.2.6. Probabilistic Programming

Some of the constraints or the objective are expressed in terms of probablistic statements about the first stage. The description of the second-stage, or recourse actions, is avoided. This is particuarly useful when the cost and benefits of second-stage decisions are difficult to asses.
